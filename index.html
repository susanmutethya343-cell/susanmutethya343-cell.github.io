Reimagining Ethiopian Coffee Culture

Reimagining Ethiopian Coffee Culture

A Journey Through Addis Ababa’s Third Wave Cafés

Coffee's origins are deeply ingrained in Ethiopian history and folklore. However, a new generation of baristas and coffee entrepreneurs in the centre of Addis Ababa is reinventing this age-old beverage by fusing modern technique with tradition to create a "third wave" coffee culture that is spreading throughout the city. This shift, which is taking place in the midst of Ethiopia's fast-paced urban pulse, is not just about coffee; it's a tale of tradition, creativity, and community.

As one strolls through the busy neighbourhoods of Addis Ababa, they come across an increasing number of modern cafés where pour-overs are prepared with exact ritual and single-origin beans are hand-roasted. In contrast to the centuries-old coffee ceremonies that continue to flourish in homes and communities, these areas are thriving centres of conversation and creativity. With an open and scientific approach that appeals to both locals and foreign connoisseurs, the third wave movement here embraces Ethiopia’s diverse coffee regions, showcasing beans from Sidamo, Harrar, and Yirgacheffe.

To understand this renaissance, I spoke with Alemayehu Tadesse, founder of Qore Coffee Roasters, who explained how his team meticulously sources beans directly from smallholder farmers to ensure quality and fair compensation. “We want to tell the story of our farmers in every cup,” he said. “This is about respect—for the land, the people, and the culture.”

Tadesse’s business is a part of a larger trend of coffee start-ups that are making significant investments in education, ranging from workshops on agronomy in rural areas to barista training. In addition to improving coffee quality, the goal is to promote economic empowerment. In certain areas, these programs have raised coffee-growing communities’ income by as much as 30%, according to a recent report by the Ethiopian Coffee and Tea Authority.

However, the change extends beyond economics. In Ethiopia, coffee is a cultural icon that invites people to relax and socialise. Customers frequently take part in live coffee ceremonies at Buna Kunda Café, a welcoming venue decorated with modern minimalism and traditional art, while sipping expertly brewed espresso shots. Selamawit Mengistu, the café’s owner, sees it as a meeting point of the modern and the ancient. “We embrace innovation while honouring our traditions,” she stated. “Every aspect of Ethiopia’s coffee story is experienced by our customers.”

Global tourists seeking a genuine taste of the place of origin are drawn to Addis Ababa’s coffee scene as tourism picks back up after the pandemic. Readers of Foodism who are organising a trip will discover a multitude of experiences here, ranging from workshops that demystify brewing techniques to farm visits that showcase the work that goes into each bean.

Whether you’re an experienced coffee lover or an inquisitive tourist, Ethiopia’s developing third wave offers a flavourful, human, and historical sensory experience. It’s a proudly complex tale that reflects Ethiopia’s own dynamic culture.

The Night I Didn’t Board the Bus That Changed My Life

The Night I Didn’t Board the Bus That Changed My Life

A Reflective Narrative by Susan Mutethya

Sometimes, the moments that alter our paths don’t come with loud warnings or grand gestures—they arrive quietly, disguised as hesitation. This is the story of one night at a Nairobi bus station when a single choice—to stay instead of run—became the turning point that reshaped everything I thought I knew about courage, destiny, and myself.

I was supposed to leave that night.

My bag was packed — one faded backpack stuffed with a change of clothes, my notebook, and a half-eaten packet of biscuits. The ticket to Mombasa was folded neatly inside my back pocket, warm against my skin as if reminding me that escape was only a bus ride away.

The 9:00 p.m. bus station in Nairobi pulsed with the chaos of movement — vendors shouting for attention, mothers herding sleepy children, drivers revving impatiently. I stood on the edge of it all, caught between leaving and staying, the night air thick with diesel fumes and indecision.

I told myself I was ready to disappear. I had rehearsed it for weeks — the goodbye note left under my mother’s cup, the silent walk through the gate, the lie I’d told my boss about visiting relatives. All I needed to do was take one step forward when the conductor called for the last passengers.

But I didn’t.

Maybe it was fear, or fatigue, or that quiet inner voice that sometimes whispers what you don’t want to hear. All I know is that my feet refused to move.

That’s when I heard him.

“Excuse me — are you okay?”

The voice was soft, curious rather than intrusive. I turned to see a man leaning against a metal pole near the edge of the platform. He wore a weathered denim jacket and had a camera slung across his chest. The streetlight above him flickered, cutting light and shadow across his face.

I nodded automatically. “I’m fine.”

He tilted his head, unconvinced. “You don’t look fine.”

I wanted to tell him to mind his business, but something in his tone — not pity, but genuine concern — disarmed me. The world around us blurred into noise: engines rumbling, horns blaring, vendors shouting about roasted maize. But between us, there was a pocket of stillness.

He offered a paper cup of tea from the small stall beside him. Steam curled into the air, carrying the sharp scent of ginger. I hesitated, then took it. The warmth seeped into my hands like an anchor.

“Running away or running toward something?” he asked.

I gave a short laugh. “Does it matter?”

“Always,” he said, and sipped his tea. “One builds you; the other breaks you.”

His name was Elijah. He was a freelance photojournalist, traveling between towns, capturing people whose lives most others overlooked. He told me stories about the faces behind his photos — fishermen mending nets by moonlight, a mother selling oranges by the highway, children chasing kites made from plastic bags.

I listened, fascinated. His words painted pictures of people who carried their struggles like medals, not burdens.

For the first time that night, I said out loud what I had been holding in for months: “I’m tired of being small. Of being expected to stay quiet, stay safe, stay the same.”

He nodded slowly, eyes focused somewhere past me. “Sometimes staying takes more courage than leaving.”

The station loudspeaker crackled. “Last call for Mombasa.”

My heart lurched. I looked toward the bus — the driver tapping the steering wheel, the conductor glancing around for stragglers. This was it.

Elijah didn’t say anything. He just watched me, his expression unreadable. The kind of silence that feels like permission, not pressure.

I clutched the ticket so tightly it creased. For a heartbeat, I imagined what would happen if I got on — the coastal air against my skin, the endless highway swallowing everything I’d left behind. But then another image came, sharper and truer: my mother waking up to an empty room, my desk at work gathering dust, the echo of my own unfinished life.

And so, I stayed.

The bus doors hissed closed, and the sound of departure was louder than I expected — a metallic sigh that felt both like relief and regret. I stood there, rooted, watching the red taillights blur into the distance until they vanished completely.

Elijah didn’t speak until the station quieted. “You can still leave tomorrow,” he said, as if sensing the guilt rising in my throat.

“Maybe,” I murmured. “Or maybe tomorrow I’ll just learn how to stay.”

He smiled, a small, knowing curve of the lips. “That’s a harder journey.”

We sat on the wooden bench as the night deepened around us. The vendors packed up their goods; the tea stall closed with a clang of metal shutters. Somewhere in the distance, a stray dog barked. Elijah showed me photos on his camera — portraits of people framed in the golden light of evening. Each one told a story without needing words.

I asked him why he took pictures of strangers.

“Because they remind me that every life is a headline if you look close enough,” he said. “And sometimes, when I feel lost, I take a picture just to remember I’m still here.”

We talked until the sky paled with dawn. I told him about the stories I used to write before life grew too loud, before fear told me my words didn’t matter. He listened the way people rarely do — fully, without waiting to reply.

When morning came, the first matatus began honking at the gate. The air smelled of wet soil and frying chapati. Elijah stood and brushed dust from his jeans.

“I should go,” he said. “There’s a story waiting somewhere on the road.”

He slung his camera over his shoulder, then paused. “Write it,” he added.

“Write what?”

“Whatever kept you from that bus.”

Then he was gone, swallowed by the rhythm of the waking city.

I sat on the bench long after he left, staring at the crushed ticket still in my hand. The ink had smudged where my fingers had pressed too hard. Around me, life carried on — people rushing, shouting, bargaining — but inside, something had shifted.

It wasn’t that I suddenly knew what I wanted. It was that I finally understood I didn’t have to run to find it.

Over the next few weeks, I went back to work, but something in me refused to settle into old routines. Each evening, I found myself writing again — fragments at first, then full scenes, the way Elijah’s photos had captured frozen moments of truth.

I wrote about the station’s flickering lights, the ginger-scented tea, the hum of engines, and that strange, still conversation that changed everything. It became my quiet rebellion against the fear of staying.

Sometimes, I caught myself wondering where Elijah was — if he was crouched behind his camera somewhere on the coast, or sitting in another station, offering tea to another lost traveler. Maybe he had already forgotten our meeting. But I hadn’t.

That night became the hinge on which my life turned — not because of the stranger himself, but because of what he mirrored back at me: the realization that I wasn’t broken or cowardly for hesitating. I was simply becoming.

It’s strange how one small act — the decision not to step forward — can rewrite your entire sense of direction. I used to believe that courage meant leaving everything behind. Now I know that sometimes it means choosing to stay and face what hurts.

A few months later, I passed by the same bus station. It looked smaller than I remembered. The metal benches were rusting, the lights dimmer, the noise somehow less intimidating.

A woman stood where I once had — clutching a ticket, eyes darting between the platform and the dark horizon. I recognized the look: that fragile mix of hope and fear. For a second, I almost walked over to her, to offer tea or words that might change her mind. But I didn’t. Her story was hers to live.

Instead, I walked past the buses, toward the city’s heartbeat — markets opening, radios crackling, people starting over again. I realized I had finally done the same.

The night I didn’t board that bus didn’t end in fireworks or grand revelations. It ended in quiet — the kind of quiet that becomes a beginning.

That night, I didn’t escape my life. I found it.

Sometimes destiny doesn’t arrive with the bus that leaves.

Sometimes it waits, quietly, with a cup of tea, for you to sit down and stay awhile.

Grains of Time: How Ancient Grains Are Shaping the Future of Food

Grains of Time

How Ancient Grains Are Shaping the Future of Food

In a time when people are obsessed with superfoods and globalised tastes, it’s easy to forget about simple foods that have fed people for thousands of years. But chefs and food historians are now looking back at ancient grains like quinoa, teff, amaranth, and millet not just because they are interesting, but also because they are colourful, long-lasting staples with a story to tell. Why now? These grains are having their moment because climate change is hurting traditional farming and people want real food. This revival isn’t just about health or taste trends. Each pearly kernel is a little piece of history, culture, and strength.

Tracing the Roots of Ancient Grains

Andean people have relied on quinoa, which is often called a “supergrain,” for a long time. People in Peru and Bolivia have been growing it for more than 5,000 years. Even though quinoa is a nutritional powerhouse, it wasn’t widely eaten in the West until the last twenty years. Teff is the small but powerful grain that makes up Ethiopian injera bread. For hundreds of years, this grain has been a staple in East Africa because it can grow in dry climates and has a lot of protein. During the global spread of industrial agriculture, which favoured high-yield monocultures, millet and amaranth, which are staples in Africa, Asia, and the Americas, were pushed aside in favour of wheat and rice. Now that people are more aware of how farming affects the environment, their ability to withstand drought and need less water is getting more attention.

Culinary Storytelling with Ancient Grains

Chef Maya Singh is a strong supporter of ancient grains in Toronto’s busy food scene. “This isn’t just a list of things,” she says. “They’re stories about communities, survival, and new ideas. When I use millet in my cooking, I’m connecting people who love food to hundreds of years of history.” Sona, her restaurant, has a signature dish that is inspired by East African flavours: millet pilaf with roasted root vegetables and a tangy tamarind sauce. “Every bite tells a story.” Singh says, “It challenges what we think grains can be.” Chefs like Maya work with local farmers to get grains that are grown in a way that doesn’t harm the environment, often heirloom varieties that are in danger of going extinct. These partnerships are part of a food movement that values taste and biodiversity.

The Science of Flavour and Nutrition

Ancient grains add different flavours and textures to a meal. Quinoa has a slightly nutty, fluffy texture; teff has an earthy, molasses-like note; and millet has a mild, slightly sweet taste that works well in both sweet and savoury dishes. These grains are often better for you than regular staples because they have more protein, fibre, and essential amino acids. Quinoa is a rare complete plant protein because it has all nine essential amino acids. Dietitians say that ancient grains are a good choice for people who can’t eat gluten and want to eat foods that are high in nutrients. But they also say that eating a variety of grains in a balanced way is important for overall health.

Cultural Preservation Through Food

Beyond food, cultural identity and self-determination are connected to the resurgence of ancient grains. In an effort to reverse centuries of agricultural marginalisation, indigenous groups in the Americas and Africa are spearheading campaigns to recover traditional crops. Quinoa cultivation in Bolivia boosts cultural pride in addition to local economies. In opposition to pressure to transition to intensive farming, organisations are assisting farmers in maintaining traditional, organic growing practices. This also has to do with culinary tourism. More and more tourists are looking for genuine culinary experiences connected to the region’s history. Ana Morales, a contributor to Foodism, recently travelled to an Andean village to observe the quinoa harvest in action. She wrote, “There is a tangible connection between land, people, and food.” “It’s a living tradition that can’t be replicated by fast food and modern agriculture.”

Challenges and Opportunities

Although demand for ancient grains is rising, it is not easy to scale up production. Market pressures and supply chain problems plague small-scale farming, which is frequently found in isolated areas. Over-commercialization is another issue. Critics pointed to price increases that endangered local food security in the Andes as quinoa gained popularity throughout the world. In addition to educating consumers about ethical sourcing, sustainability advocates support fair trade models that help farming communities. Customers and chefs can contribute at home by supporting producers and chefs who are dedicated to sustainable practices and broadening their grain selections.

A Grain for the Future

Ancient grains provide a resilient way forward as climate unpredictability increases. Their rich cultural and gastronomic heritage can encourage a more deliberate, environmentally friendly way of eating. “Embracing ancient grains is about honouring the past while adapting for the future—one plate at a time,” says Maya Singh.

Rewilding the Urban Soul: How Indigenous Voices Are Reshaping Climate Justice in Our Cities

Rewilding the Urban Soul

How Indigenous Voices Are Reshaping Climate Justice in Our Cities

Cities are battlefields of ecological crisis and cultural survival beneath the din of traffic and the glass facades of the skyline. Communities that are directly impacted by environmental injustice and climate change are disproportionately found in urban areas. However, a story of transformation is emerging within this struggle: marginalised and Indigenous voices are taking back urban spaces and bringing with them their culture, ancestral knowledge, and hope for a sustainable future. By examining how climate, culture, and justice intersect in urban parks, streets, and neighbourhoods, this study unearths tales of inventiveness, resiliency, and identity that subvert prevailing environmental paradigms.

Significantly higher pollution levels are found in neighbourhoods with a preponderance of Black, Indigenous, and People of Colour (BIPOC) residents, according to air quality monitors in many American cities. Heat islands and industrial runoff plague Minneapolis’s neighbourhoods around the Mississippi River, which was once the Dakota people’s ancestral home. Echoing a sombre legacy of systemic neglect, these environmental factors contribute to increased rates of cardiovascular disease and asthma. The Environmental Protection Agency’s (EPA) investigative data validates the disparities in the environment. These vulnerable communities are disproportionately located close to highways, waste incinerators, and industrial facilities. However, in conversations about urban planning, the opinions of those affected are frequently ignored.

“We are living on land that was taken and poisoned,” says Maria Torres, a local Indigenous organiser, describing the emotional toll. “Our stories are erased, but our people get sick. Climate justice entails recovering our history and dignity in addition to having clean air.”

Indigenous communities see cities as living ecosystems that can be restored, despite their outward appearance of being rigid and concrete. Through interviews and field visits, it became clear that Indigenous urban gardens and sacred spaces serve as critical hubs of ecological restoration and cultural renewal. For instance, the Dakota-led “Roots of Resistance” youth program teaches resilience to seedlings in Minneapolis’s parks by fusing traditional plant knowledge with contemporary activism. While young people cultivate native species that improve soil health and attract pollinators, elders tell stories about the medicinal uses of the plants. The contrast between the green shoots and the surrounding urban greyness in a series of photographs capturing these gardens serves as a visual metaphor for hope in the face of hardship.

Personal essays from Indigenous and local community members who expressed a strong bond between their cultural identity and climate activism were included in the study. “The drought in my parents’ village disrupted more than just crops—it disrupted our traditions and community fabric,” says Tala Anaya, a Maya writer who lives in Los Angeles. “Despite feeling dispersed by the vast city, I managed to cultivate native plants at urban farms. In the face of climate change, it’s a way for me to pay tribute to my ancestors.” These stories demonstrate the profound effects of climate change on artistic expression, self-awareness, and communal cohesion. They also emphasise the importance of storytelling in maintaining threatened cultural memory through written language, oral tradition, and visual art.

Photo essays and creative nonfiction are effective means of bridging the gap between data and human experience, undermining prevailing narratives that separate city dwellers from the natural world. For example, the “Sacred Ground” project documents Indigenous-led ecological restoration in American cities through mixed-media installations and large-format photography. The pictures highlight resilience and interdependence by showing not only ecology but also cultural rituals and social events. This work’s exhibits have spurred community discussion and persuaded decision-makers to reevaluate the design of urban green spaces. Art transforms into activism by providing concrete examples of the paths to sustainability and equity.

Emerging Indigenous and minority-led businesses that combine innovation and traditional knowledge for environmental justice were found through the investigation. Under the direction of Navajo engineer Leila Yazzie, the startup “EcoSage” develops affordable water purification systems for underprivileged communities and community gardens in places like Albuquerque and Phoenix. EcoSage bridges the gap between technology and tradition by teaching culturally relevant water stewardship. In a similar vein, Marcus Nwosu, an architect based in Detroit, turns abandoned lots into green spaces that combine affordable housing, food production, and cultural venues. His creations are the epitome of sustainability, cultural affirmation, and community empowerment. These trailblazers serve as examples of how urban sustainability can be economically feasible, inclusive, and culturally grounded.

This study comes to the conclusion that urban climate solutions that are separated from environmental justice and cultural identity are insufficient. “True sustainability must restore relationships—to each other, to land, and to history,” emphasise locals like Maria Torres, Tala Anaya, and the communities they represent. Investing in culturally sensitive innovations, acknowledging Indigenous stewardship within city limits, and elevating marginalised storytellers are all necessary to restore the urban ecosystem. The city is now a place for rewilded futures rather than just survival. These leaders encourage us all to rethink our cities as dynamic ecosystems where nature and culture come together to work towards justice, in their own words.
